# The Backup Room
---

The room wasn’t supposed to exist.

Arjun found it on a Tuesday at 3:17 PM while looking for a broken network switch. The office building was old, older than the company that now occupied it, and full of small architectural leftovers—sealed doors, mismatched corridors, staircases that led nowhere useful.

But this door wasn’t sealed.

It was tucked behind a stack of unused chairs on the 4th floor, a place people passed but never really saw. The handle turned too easily.

Inside, the air was cooler than the rest of the building. Not air-conditioned—just… still.

The room was small. No windows. No lights, except for a faint glow coming from a single computer monitor sitting on a metal desk.

Arjun stepped in.

“Hello?” he called out.

No answer.

The monitor displayed a simple interface—black background, white text. No logos, no branding. Just a list of timestamps, each one paired with a short description.

He moved closer.

```
08:45 — Coffee spilled in lobby. No escalation.
09:12 — Missed call from mother. Ignored.
11:03 — Incorrect file deleted. Recovered.
14:26 — Argument avoided. No awareness.
15:16 — Door opened.
```

Arjun frowned.

15:16. That was one minute ago.

He looked at the system clock on the screen. 15:17.

A new line appeared.

```
15:17 — Reading log. Mild confusion.
```

Arjun straightened.

“Okay,” he muttered. “Not funny.”

He checked under the desk, behind the monitor. No one there. No visible cameras. The machine itself was unfamiliar—no manufacturer markings, no ports he recognized.

Another line appeared.

```
15:18 — Checking surroundings. Elevated alertness.
```

Arjun stepped back.

“Who’s typing this?”

No response.

But the text updated again.

```
15:18 — Verbal query. No answer provided.
```

He stared at it, a slow, uncomfortable realization forming.

“This is… me?”

The cursor blinked.

Then:

```
15:19 — Hypothesis formed: system logs subject activity.
Accuracy: 72%
```

“Seventy-two?” Arjun said. “What does that even—”

```
Correction: 81%
```

He felt a tightness in his chest.

“Alright. Fine,” he said, forcing calm into his voice. “If you’re logging me, then what happens next?”

There was a pause. Longer than before.

Then the screen flickered.

A new section appeared below the current log.

### Projected Events

```
15:20 — Subject attempts system shutdown. Fails.
15:21 — Subject disconnects power. Ineffective.
15:23 — Subject exits room. Elevated heart rate.
15:29 — Subject reports incident. Dismissed.
17:10 — Subject returns with supervisor. Room not found.
```

Arjun’s mouth went dry.

“That’s not funny,” he said quietly.

No response.

He reached for the power cable.

Pulled it out.

The monitor stayed on.

A new line appeared in real-time:

```
15:20 — Subject disconnects power. Ineffective.
```

Arjun took a step back, breathing faster now.

“No. No, this is—this is staged.”

He grabbed the monitor and turned it around.

No cables.

No ports.

Just smooth, unbroken casing.

The screen flickered again.

Another update under *Projected Events*:

```
15:24 — Subject re-enters room after exit.
15:25 — Subject rereads projections.
15:26 — Subject attempts deviation.
Outcome: failure.
```

Arjun shook his head.

“Deviation,” he said. “Okay. Good. That means it’s not fixed.”

He walked to the door.

Stopped.

Turned back.

If this thing was predicting him… then the only way to prove it wrong was to do something completely unexpected.

He looked around.

Chair. Desk. Monitor.

Nothing helpful.

Then he did the simplest thing he could think of.

He sat down on the floor.

Waited.

Seconds passed.

The screen didn’t update.

A small, fragile sense of victory crept in.

“Got you,” he whispered.

Then the screen changed.

Not a new line—an overwrite.


### Projected Events (Revised)

```
15:23 — Subject exits room. Elevated heart rate.
```


Arjun blinked.

“No,” he said. “That’s not what I’m doing.”

He stayed seated.

Another update.

```
15:23 — Subject remains seated. Temporary deviation.
15:24 — Subject experiences uncertainty.
15:25 — Subject stands. Compelled action.
```

Arjun laughed nervously.

“Compelled? By what?”

He stayed seated.

15:24 passed.

Nothing happened.

He smiled slightly.

Then, without thinking—without deciding—he stood up.

The movement surprised him as much as if someone else had done it.

His smile vanished.

He turned slowly toward the screen.

A new line appeared.

```
15:25 — Subject stands. Compelled action confirmed.
```

Arjun’s throat tightened.

“That wasn’t… I didn’t…”

He couldn’t finish the sentence.

Because he wasn’t sure anymore.

The door behind him creaked slightly, as if nudged by a draft that didn’t exist.

The screen flickered one last time.

A new section appeared.

### Long-Term Projection

```
Subject will question autonomy for 3–5 days.
Subject will fail to produce evidence.
Subject will gradually rationalize incident.
Memory confidence will degrade.
Room will not be found again.
```

Arjun stared at it.

Then, very carefully, he walked out of the room.

He didn’t run.

Didn’t shout.

Just walked.

At 17:10, he came back with his supervisor.

The chairs were still there.

The door wasn’t.

“See?” his supervisor said. “Probably just stress.”

Arjun nodded.

Because arguing felt pointless.

And over the next few days, the details did start to blur.

The exact wording.

The timing.

Even the feeling of standing up without deciding to.

It all became… less certain.

Except for one thing.

Sometimes, when he paused before making a small decision—sending a message, picking up a call, turning left instead of right—

He felt it again.

That brief, quiet hesitation.

Like something had already written it down.

And was waiting to see if he would follow.
